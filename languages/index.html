With more than 8.5 million residents, New York City is one of the most diverse metropolis in the world. The 2016 American Community Survey identified the presence of more than 40 languages in the city.

According to the data, and without much surprise, English is the most spoken language in New York City. But let's take a closer look.



English Spanish Yiddish Chinese Other Hindi

This is what happens when we strip New York City from its residents who speak English at home. Once again, no surprise: Spanish is the second-most spoken language in the city.

However, a few communities start to appear on the map, for example Chinese speakers, who are represented in light green, and Yiddish speakers in bright blue.



Spanish Yiddish Chinese Other Hindi French Creole Greek French Polish Italian

If we take away the layer of Spanish speakers, we are left with dozens of language communities. It's slightly dizzying, so let's start by taking a closer look at one neighborhood.



Yiddish Chinese Other Hindi French Creole Greek French Polish Italian Russian Urdu Japanese Korean Tagalog Hungarian Portuguese Serbo-croatian Arabic

As we were taking English and Spanish speakers out of the picture, the Greek community of Astoria, Queens, became more visible, so it seems like a good place to start.


Astoria, Queens

Greek immigrants started to settle in Astoria after the end of World War II. Before that, the neighborhood used to be home to mostly Italian residents. In the 90s, Greeks accounted for about half of Astoria residents, and this number has decreased since then. The area is culturally very mixed, but only 4% of Astoria residents speak Greek at home, according to 2016 ACS data.

The neighborhood is expectedly undergoing gentrification, and the average price per square foot jumped in 2003.

Let's look at what language diversity looks like in Astoria, according to 2016 ACS data.

These are the many languages of Astoria, and their breakdown.

English and Spanish are dominating, as this can easily be seen. Many languages such as French, Tagalog or Chinese are each spoken by about 1% of Astoria residents.

Greek is the third most spoken language, but only accounts for 4% of the total population of the neighborhood.

Now let's try to find a neighborhood where the most spoken language is neither English, nor Spanish. But if we think about the map we had at the very beginning, one language seemed to stand out: Yiddish.

Let's find a Yiddish speaking neighborhood.


Borough Park, Brooklyn

Borough Park is home to one of the largest communities of Orthodox Jews in the United States, and most of its residents are Hasids - a specific branch of Orthodox Judaism.

As a result, most residents speak Yiddish at home, and someone strolling around the area will almost exclusively encounter signs written in this language, or in Hebrew - except on Shabbat, every Friday and Saturday: streets are deserted and stores are all closed.

Instead of directly looking at Borough Park as it is now, let's go back in time and see what the neighborhood was like in 1970.

THIS

English Yiddish Italian Spanish Hungarian German Polish French Russian Others / NR

This was Borough Park's language breakdown in 1970, according to Decennial Census data.

If English was, as usual, the most spoken language, Yiddish came second, with 22% of the neighborhood's residents speaking it at home.

Interestingly, Italians were also very present, and 21% of the residents spoke Italian at home.

Let's see how Borough Park has changed.

This is Borough Park today, according to 2016 ACS data.

Yiddish has taken over English and all other languages.

Italian has disappeared (less than 1% of the inhabitants of the neighborhood speak it at home), but Spanish and Chinese are on the rise.

Now let's pause for a second. How could we get a broader view of the city's language variety, and how could we measure where the city is most linguistically diverse?

Enters Greenberg's Linguistic Diversity Index (LDI)

The LDI is a number between 0 and 1, where 0 represents a very homogenous neighborhood, and 1 a very diverse area.


New York City's Language Diversity Index,
per census tract

To understand what this means, let's compare two census tracts: one very diverse, with a LDI close to 1, and one not diverse at all, with a LDI close to 0.

A perfect pair seems to be Census Tract 916.01 (LDI of 0.047) and Census Tract 747 (LDI of 0.88), both located in Queens, home to 3800 and 3263 residents respectively, according to the 2016 five-years ACS. Only languages that have more than 30 or more speakers are represented in the charts that follow.


Census Tract 747, Queens.

LDI: 0.88

Census Tract 916.01, Queens

LDI: 0.047


Census Tract 747, Queens, has a large diversity of languages. If the most spoken one is Chinese, the second most-spoken one is not English, but Russian. English comes third, closely followed by Hindi and Spanish.

On the contrary, 3709 out of the 3800 residents of Census Tract 916.01, Queens, speak English. In fact, more than 95% of its residents are white, making it one of the least diverse areas in the city.

Data for 2016 comes from the 2016 ACS 5 years survey. Data for 1970 comes from the 1970 Decennial Census, adjusted for 2010 geographies. The code is available on Github.
